1. Executive Summary

NeoWallet aims to be a "social banking" app for Gen Z — mobile-only, with shared savings pots, public transaction feeds, and squad-based financial goals. The business model proposes a $5/month subscription.

Analysis of 6 source documents (initial brief, 3 user interviews, 1 competitor benchmark, 1 technical constraints document) reveals fundamental tensions between the product vision and available evidence:

  • Privacy vs Social: The core differentiator (public finance) is exactly what users resist. "Money is private. It's creepy." (CF-03 — needs research)
  • Pricing: $5/month is contradicted by all user and market data. Users expect free or $2-3 max. No successful competitor combines subscription + social. (CF-04 — needs research)
  • Tech stack: "100% native" is not feasible for the 3-month timeline. CEO approved Cordova/React wrapper with Flutter migration post-PMF. (CF-02 — resolved)

Bottom line: Only 1 of 4 product modules (Core Banking) can be fully specified today. The social layer and monetization model require stakeholder decisions and additional research before they can be defined.

2. User Research Findings

Three user interviews were conducted. Key findings by theme:

2.1 Privacy Expectations

"I honestly find it creepy when people broadcast what they pay for. I distinctly remember my friend getting roasted for spending $50 on a game skin." — Alex [IG-07]

Alex keeps Venmo transactions private. The assumption that Gen Z wants public financial data is directly challenged. This affects the entire Social Layer module.

2.2 Price Sensitivity

"Why would I pay a subscription just to hold my own money?" — Alex [IG-08]
"I'd pay for good features. Maybe $2 or $3. $5 feels steep." — Taylor [IG-10]

Two users independently signal that $5/month is above willingness-to-pay. Free alternatives (Chime, CashApp) set the baseline expectation.

2.3 Trust & Security

"Would you link your main bank account?" — "Hell no. Security risk. I'd only put 'play money' in there." — Jordan [IG-12]

Only one source addresses trust, but the signal is strong. Onboarding design must not require primary bank account linking.

Evidence gap: Only 3 interviews conducted. Sample is too small for definitive conclusions. Directional signals are consistent but need validation at scale.
