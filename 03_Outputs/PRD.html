1. Product Overview

NeoWallet is a mobile-only "social banking" experience targeting Gen Z users. The core premise is multiplayer finance — shared savings pots, public transaction feeds, and squad-based financial goals — designed to make personal finance viral and social rather than solitary and boring.

The product faces fundamental tensions between its social-first vision and strong user resistance to public financial data, as well as a pricing model contradicted by all available market and user evidence. These tensions are unresolved and block two of the four product modules.

Sources: [IG-01], [IG-03]

2. User Problem & Context

Gen Z users find traditional banking apps boring and impersonal. They manage finances reactively ("I look at my balance before I buy") and resist tools that feel like work (manual budgeting, spreadsheets). There is appetite for a more engaging, mobile-native experience.

However, user research surfaces three critical friction points:

  • Privacy: Users find public financial broadcasting "creepy." They keep Venmo transactions private and don't want friends seeing their spending. [IG-07]
  • Price sensitivity: Free alternatives exist (Chime, CashApp). Users are unwilling to pay $5/month; $2-3 is the ceiling, and many expect free. [IG-08], [IG-10]
  • Trust: Users refuse to link main bank accounts due to security concerns. They'd only put "play money" in a new app. [IG-12]

5. Module Specifications

5.1 Core Banking Verified

The banking foundation layer. Handles account management, transactions, and settlement through the OldGuard SOAP API.

Key Decision (CF-02 Resolved): CEO confirmed Cordova/React wrapper for 3-month launch timeline. The brief's "100% native" mandate is deferred — the backlog must include an approximate milestone for Flutter migration after reaching product-market fit. [IG-06] wins for MVP, [IG-03] remains long-term target

Technical constraints:

  • OldGuard SOAP API with T+2 day settlement latency. The "Stark" API mentioned in the brief is not compliance-approved and won't be available for at least 12 months. [IG-05]
  • React web portal wrapped in Cordova for iOS/Android. Not native — accepted trade-off for speed to market. [IG-06]
  • 1 API call/second rate limit per user — impacts real-time multiplayer features.
  • Social graph data must live on separate infrastructure from banking data (PCI-DSS).

Implications:

  • Real-time transaction feeds are impossible with OldGuard. Any social feed showing transactions must display "pending" states and tolerate 2-day latency. (See CF-01 in Open Questions.)
  • Cordova wrapper limits UX quality — no native gestures, slower animations, larger bundle size.

5.2 Social Layer Blocked on CF-03

Blocked: This module cannot be fully specified until CF-03 (Social vs Privacy) is resolved through additional user research on privacy expectations in social finance. Design must assume opt-in social, private by default until stakeholders decide on positioning. [IG-01] vs [IG-07]

The brief's core differentiator is "multiplayer finance" — shared savings pots, public transaction feeds, and squad-based goals. However, user research directly contradicts the assumption that Gen Z wants public financial data:

"Money is private. I honestly find it creepy when people broadcast what they pay for." — Alex (user interview) [IG-07]

What we can specify now:

  • Shared savings pots (private between participants) — no privacy conflict.
  • Squad goals (opt-in groups) — acceptable if membership is by invitation.

What requires resolution:

  • Public transaction feeds — the brief's key differentiator but directly opposed by user evidence.
  • Default visibility settings — private-by-default vs social-by-default fundamentally changes the product identity.

5.3 Monetization Blocked on CF-04

Blocked: The proposed $5/month subscription is contradicted by all available evidence. This module cannot be specified until CF-04 is resolved through freemium benchmarking and stakeholder alignment on pricing strategy. [IG-04] vs [IG-08], [IG-10], [IG-11]

The brief proposes a flat $5/month subscription, citing Gen Z's preference for transparency over ads. Three independent sources contradict this:

Source Finding Ref
User interview (Alex) "Why would I pay a subscription just to hold my own money?" — Free alternatives exist. [IG-08]
User interview (Taylor) Willing to pay $2-3. "$5 feels steep unless it comes with heavy perks." [IG-10]
Competitor benchmark No successful app combines subscription AND social features. Market bifurcates: free/social or paid/privacy. [IG-11]

Research needed before specifying: Freemium conversion rate benchmarks in fintech, ARPU analysis of comparable apps, alternative monetization models (transaction fees, partnerships, premium cosmetics).

5.4 Trust & Security Needs research

Users exhibit a fundamental trust barrier with new fintech apps. Jordan explicitly refuses to link their main bank account, calling it a "security risk" — willing to deposit only "play money."

Key Insight: "Would you link your main bank account?" — "Hell no. Security risk. I'd only put like 'play money' in there." [IG-12]

Design implications:

  • Onboarding cannot require primary bank account linking. A "play money" deposit flow may be necessary.
  • Trust must be earned incrementally — start with low-risk features, progressively suggest deeper integration.
  • Security messaging must be prominent — PCI-DSS compliance, data separation, encryption — without being patronizing.

Gap: Only one source (Jordan) addresses trust. The brief ignores security perception entirely. Additional user research is needed to understand whether this is a universal barrier or a segment-specific concern.

6. Design Principles

  1. Privacy by default. Social features must be opt-in. Never expose transactions without explicit consent. Users find financial broadcasting "creepy." [IG-07], [IG-12]
  2. Ship fast, iterate later. Cordova MVP to validate market fit. Flutter migration after PMF, not before. Avoid premature optimization. [IG-06], CF-02
  3. Price-sensitive audience. Gen Z won't pay $5. Whatever monetization model is chosen, it must respect a $0-3 willingness range or offer exceptional perceived value. [IG-08], [IG-10], [IG-11]
